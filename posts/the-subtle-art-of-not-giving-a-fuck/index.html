The Subtle Art Of Not Giving A Fuck

Uploaded November 30th, 2021

Short: Reorganize your values and choose wisely about what you give a fuck about in life.

Chapter 1: Don’t Try

Stop trying. You become poor when you try to be rich.  

“Ironically, this fixation on the positive only serves to remind us over and over again of what we are not, of what we lack, of what we should have been but failed to be.”

Either you are, or you are not. So don’t try to be somebody you’re not.

“The key to a good life is not giving a fuck about more; it’s giving a fuck about less, giving a fuck about only what is true and immediate and important.”

And that’s basically what the book is about: Choose what to give a fuck about. Dedicate your fucks only to what’s truly fuck-worthy. Choose what’s important to you and give a fuck about that. 

“Subtlety #1: Not giving a fuck does not mean being indifferent; it means being comfortable with being different.”

“Subtlety #2: To not give a fuck about adversity, you must first give a fuck about something more important than adversity.”

“Subtlety #3: Whether you realize it or not, you are always choosing what to give a fuck about.”

Short: Stop caring, stop trying, and just live and be. 

Chapter 2: Happiness Is a Problem

The belief that happiness is algorithmic "if I achieve X, then I can be happy" is the problem. Happiness is not a solvable equation.

“The secret sauce is in the solving of problems, not in not having problems in the first place.”

Mark Manson tells this story of himself wanting to be a rock star. He dreamed about it and imagined himself being on stage with thousands of people yelling his name and stuff. But he never even came close. Why? Because he didn’t want it. “I wanted the reward and not the struggle. I wanted the result and not the process. I was in love with not the fight but only the victory.”

Short: Only when you enjoy the journey will you reach your destination.

Chapter 3: You Are Not Special

Fuck positivity. Sometimes things are fucked up, and we need to live with it. Yeah, it’s probably better to see the glass half-full than half-empty. But sometimes, the glass is full of shit. You cannot change that by being positive about it.

We’ve become a generation that feels entitled. Entitled people feel that they deserve good things without actually earning them. Entitlement isn’t impervious. People who are entitled delude themselves into whatever feeds their sense of superiority.”

Entitled people only see their positives. They can’t acknowledge their problems and weaknesses. And sooner or later, reality will hit, and it will be painful because reality is not a unicorn dancing on clouds. Life sucks.

The vast majority of life is unextraordinary, indeed quite average. There’s nothing wrong with trying to become exceptional at something. However, you need to realize first that you’re only mediocre. And you need to work extremely hard to get there. You’re not there yet. Not even near.

Short: Your average, with no special gift/talent at all. So outwork everyone.

Chapter 4: The Value of Suffering

Marathon runners run through a lot of shit. Every day they get up and run a lot. That’s the shit they choose. Teachers go through a lot of shit as well, and so does everyone else. So, if you have to go through a lot of shit, which shit do you prefer?

The Self-Awareness Onion.

“Self-awareness is like an onion. There are multiple layers to it, and the more you peel them back, the more likely you’re going to start crying at inappropriate times.”

The author offers a simple system to find out why something bothers you. Ask why. Keep on asking why (Why does this seem true?).

Shitty Values

Pleasure is not the cause of happiness; rather, it is the effect—the most superficial form of life satisfaction, the easiest to obtain, and the easiest to lose.

Material success. “When people measure themselves not by their behavior, but by the status symbols they’re able to collect, then not only are they shallow, but they’re probably assholes as well.”

Always Being Right. “The fact is, people who base their self-worth on being right about everything prevent themselves from learning from their mistakes… It’s far more helpful to assume that you’re ignorant and don’t know a whole lot. This keeps you unattached to superstitious or poorly informed beliefs and promotes a constant state of learning and growth.”

Staying Positive "Things go wrong, people upset us, accidents happen. These things make us feel like shit. And that’s fine. Negative emotions are a necessary component of emotional health. To deny that negativity is to perpetuate problems rather than solve them.”

What Are Good Values?

  1. reality-based
  2. socially constructive
  3. immediate and controllable

“Some examples of good, healthy values: honesty, innovation, vulnerability, standing up for oneself, standing up for others, self-respect, curiosity, charity, humility, creativity.”

This is what self-improvement is about: prioritizing better values, choosing better things to give a fuck about. Because when you give better fucks, you get better problems. And when you get better problems, you get a better life.”

Chapter 5:You Are Always Choosing

If we realize that we’re responsible for everything in our lives, then we get to improve ourselves. No matter the hand you’re dealt, it’s your responsibility to deal with the hand.

Many people may be to blame for your unhappiness, but nobody is ever responsible for your unhappiness but you. This is because you always get to choose how you see things, how you react to things, how you value things.

“Often, the only difference between a problem being painful or being powerful is a sense that we chose it and that we are responsible for it.”

Chapter 6: You’re Wrong About Everything

A while back, people were certain that things that we know today are complete BS. For example, people believed that the world was flat. The same holds for the younger version of you.

“Certainty is the enemy of growth.”

In most situations, we get an intuition that says something is good or bad. But in reality, we don’t know. It seems a certain way, but we don’t know. So maybe it’s good, and perhaps it’s terrible. How about taking it as it comes? It’s okay how it is. Maybe it’ll benefit us, and perhaps it’ll harm us. We simply do not know for sure.

Question #1: What if I’m wrong?

“As a general rule, we’re all the world’s worst observers of ourselves. So when we’re angry, or jealous, or upset, we’re oftentimes the last ones to figure it out.”

Question #2: What would it mean if I were wrong?

In most cases, the answer would be something like this

“If I’m wrong, then I’m behaving like a total asshole.”

Question #3: Would being wrong create better/worse problems than my current problem?

Are we just being a selfish idiot, or is there something to what we believe?

“That’s simply reality: if it feels like it’s you versus the world, chances are it’s really just you versus yourself.”

Chapter 7: Failure Is the Way Forward

Talent is overrated. It's more about the work you put into something rather than your preconditions. If you want to succeed at something, you will undoubtedly have to fail many times. So failure and pain are part of the process.

The "Do Something" Principle

"'If you're stuck on a problem, don't just sit there and think about it; start working on it. Even if you don't know what you're doing, the simple act of working on it will eventually cause the right ideas to show up in your head.'"

Don't just sit there. Do something. The answers will follow. Action isn't just the effect of motivation; it's also the cause of it.

"If you lack the motivation to make an important change in your life, do something – anything, really – and then harness the reaction to that action as a way to begin motivating yourself."

Short: It is better to do something and fail than nothing at all.

Chapter 8: The Importance of Saying No

People often say yes to things they don't want to please others.

"The point is this: we all must give a fuck about something to value something. And to value something, we must reject what is not that something. To value X, we must reject non-X."

Chapter 9: And Then You Die

"Death scares us. And because it scares us, we avoid thinking about it, talking about it, sometimes even acknowledging it, even when it's happening to someone close to us.

  1. Death Terror: At some point, humans become aware of the inevitability of death. This leads to deep existential anxiety that underlies everything we think or do.
  2. Immortality Projects: Humans have two selves: a physical self that eats, sleeps, and a conceptual self which is how we see ourselves. As we know that our physical self is mortal, we try to ensure that our conceptual self will not be forgotten.

"The only way to be comfortable with death is to understand and see yourself as something bigger than yourself; to choose values that stretch beyond serving yourself. This is the basic root of all happiness."

But because we feel entitled, and that we are the center of the universe, that we suffer most, and that we deserve greatness more than anybody else, because of that we are not comfortable with death. And that's why we do not truly live.

​In short:

​Prioritize your fucks wisely.
